## The Magnetic Compass

![The cutout of a magnetic compass](/images/compasscutout.png "The cutout of a magnetic compass")

This is a cutaway of a magnetic compass. Now, notice how it's constructed. The compass card is what we call a horizontal card and it's sitting on a pivot point. We *fill this compass with a clear fluid* much like kerosene, and it dampens the movement of that card as it sits on this pivot point.

So if you take a careful look at that cutaway across the back, you're going to see a rubber diaphragm, and then across the front, of course, you're going to see a clear glass so pilots can see the card. At the top, you're going to see a filler point where a mechanic could drain or refill the fluid inside the compass, and you're also going to see what we call compensating magnets. Now, those compensating magnets are for an airframe mechanic to help determine the compass's deviation.

Now, the final thing I want you to see in this big cutaway is that card is sitting on a pivot point, and underneath of that card are two magnets. *Those magnets will always align themselves with the flux lines of the earth's magnetic field.* 

Now, if you take a look at this exaggerated picture, as we get closer to the north or south poles, that compass card is going to want to tilt and dip to follow those flux lines. **The way that we avoid that with the magnetic compass is there are counterweights down below this compass card to help that card stay level at more northern or southern latitudes.** Now, if we get close enough to either the north or south pole, that tipping tendency is going to be so great that the magnetic compass becomes essentially useless, approximately 300 miles of the pole, so this instrument is very effective across most of the planet.

**The beauty of the magnetic compass is it is not powered by anything**; however, the disadvantage is this compass comes with some errors that we have to deal with.


### Understanding the Compass Card
So before we get into those errors, let's look at the face of this compass card. Now, I want you to take a look at the face of this card. Notice right in front of us is a big N with the line through it. That line is called a lubber line. That's an old nautical term, and the lubber line is showing me the heading on this card. So in our example, notice the thin lubber line is going through that large capital letter N. Looking at this compass card, that means I would be traveling to the north.

If you look closely at this card, off to the right, the numbers indicate northwest and eventually west, and off to the left of the card, the numbers indicate northeast and eventually east. Now, that's kind of backwards. Well, it's backwards on the card, but it's not really backwards. **We are looking at the back side of a horizontal compass card**. So, in fact, I am traveling zero-three-zero—the card's telling me I'm going zero-three-zero—but I am looking at the southwest side of this horizontal compass card if I were to look at it with a bird's eye view. So that's the first consideration about magnetic compasses. 

It's not really an error; it's just something we have to be conscious of because of the way that it was built.

## Magnetic Compass Errors
Now let's take a look at the common magnetic compass errors. We generally break this down into four types of errors. The first two errors, variation and deviation, these are not necessarily an error of the compass itself, but these are errors caused because of the way we want to use the compass and where we locate the compass.

### 1. Variation
>  **Variation is the difference between the true and the magnetic poles at a given point.** 

The true North Pole is an imaginary spot where we have drawn lines of longitude that connect the north and south pole of the planet. These imaginary lines of longitude connect at true north and true south. Well, in addition to that, we have magnetic north and magnetic south. Now, these are not man-made; these poles are a result of the fact that the earth has an iron core. Iron is a ferrous material, which means it's very magnetic, and this causes our planet to have a magnetic field.

These magnetic *flux lines* run into the north pole and out of the south pole, making this magnetic field. *That magnetic north pole is not at the same location as the true north pole*. Now, **if I were to take a line from the true north pole and draw it straight south through the magnetic north pole, that line we would call the agonic line.**  What that means is, if I was in my airplane flying to magnetic north on the agonic line, I would need zero correction to go straight north to true north.

![Agonic line](/images/agonicline.png "Agonic line")

You can see an agonic line depicted on your chart with **magenta dashed lines** that says 7W,  7 degrees west. What it means is we are east of the agonic line and we need 7 degrees of west variation correction. 

![Isogonic linees](/images/isogonicline.png "Isogonic linees")

### 2. Deviation
> **Deviation is a compass error that occurs due to disturbances from magnetic fields produced by metals and electrical accessories within the airplane.**

Deviation is caused by things in your aircraft that will affect a magnetic field or create a magnetic field, and that's going to make those little magnets in that compass—we looked at those earlier—it's going to make those little magnets deviate.

So the way we correct for this is a mechanic will take this aircraft and park it out on the airport on a known magnetic heading, turn everything on in the airplane—engine, radios, etc.—and record how much deviation this compass has on that heading. *Your airframe mechanic will then write that deviation down on this little card. It's called a compass deviation card*. Up at the top, it shows the actual compass heading, and then right below it, it shows the deviation of that compass. And notice in our example, it shows it radios on and radios off. Okay, so that's deviation and that's a compass deviation card.

### *(Magnetic Dip)*
> **When the bar magnet contained in the compass is pulled by the earth’s magnetic field, it tends to point north and somewhat downward, which is called the magnetic dip, contributing to acceleration and turning errors.** 

![Magnetic Dip](/images/magdip.png "Magnetic Dip")

Within approximately *300 miles* of either magnetic pole, these errors are so great that use of the compass for navigation is impractical. 
### 3. Acceleration Error
> **The magnetic dip and the forces of inertia cause magnetic
compass errors when accelerating and decelerating on
easterly and westerly headings (ANDS)**

![Acceleration Error](/images/accelerationerror.png "Acceleration Error")

When the aircraft accelerates—and you can see this depicted by the red dashed lines here in our diagram—the aft end of that compass sort of picks up, and when it does that, the compass swings to a northerly heading. That's an acceleration error.

Now, why is it swinging like that? Remember when we first talked about the wet compass or the magnetic compass and we talked about that card sitting on the pivot and we talked about the fact that it's got some counterweights to help stabilize that card? The interaction between these magnets and those counterweights cause that card to turn toward the north when we accelerate. 

When we decelerate—Notice in our diagram—this is the wavy yellow line here—the forward end of the card lifts up and those magnets and those weights interact, and the compass turns toward a southerly heading. 

> [!TIPS]
> Acceleration error mnemonic: **ANDS**\
> **A** : Accelerate\
> **N** : North\
> **D** : Decelerate\
> **S** : South\

### 4. Turning Errors
> **When you begin a turn from a heading of north, the compass initially indicates a turn in the opposite direction. When the turn is established, the compass begins to turn in the correct direction, but it lags behind the actual heading. The amount of lag decreases as the turn continues, then disappears as the airplane reaches a heading of east or west. (UNOS)**

![Turning Error](/images/turningerror.png "Turning Error")

To understand dip errors, let's go back to that cutaway and remind ourselves: *the magnets want to stay in line with those flux lines, but the weights help to stabilize that card to keep that card level as we head north and south.*

If we're on a northerly heading and we turn the aircraft to a southerly heading, as we turn towards the south, that card will dip with the weights and the magnets will try to follow those flux lines during the time the aircraft is turning, and the card will turn past the heading we need to get to—heading south. So we have to lead the rollout. That's what we mean when we say **towards the south, the compass leads.**

Now, the opposite happens when we turn towards a northerly heading. When we turn **towards a northerly heading, the card lags.** And it's the own thing: the airplane is turning north and the card starts to swing; the magnets want to follow the flux line, but the weights below that card are trying to hold it level, and those interact to cause this—this northerly turning dip error. 


> [!TIPS]
> Turning error mnemonic: **UNOS**\
> **U** : Undershoot \
> **N** : North (Stop the turn before reaching the North heading)\
> **O** : Overshoot \
> **S** : South (Pass the South heading before stopping the turn)
