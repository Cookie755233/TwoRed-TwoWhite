## Stalls
> **Stalls are a rapid decrease in lift caused by a separation of airflow from the wing's surface brought on by exceeding the critical angle of attack.**
![Increasing the angle of attack beyond C Lmax causes progressive disruption of airflow from the upper surface of the wing.](/images/stall.png "Increasing the angle of attack beyond C Lmax causes progressive disruption of airflow from the upper surface of the wing.")

> [!CAUTION]
> It’s important to understand the variables that affect stall development. As indicated earlier in this chapter, a stall will always occur when the maximum lift, or critical angle of attack (C<sub>LMAX</sub> ) is exceeded. 

### Factors Affecting Stall Speed
1. **Weight**\
   As aircraft weight increases, a higher angle of attack is required to maintain the same airspeed because some of the lift must be used to support the increased weight. This causes an increase in the aircraft’s stall speed.
2. **Center of gravity (CG)**\
   a forward CG creates a situation that requires the tail to produce more downforce to balance the aircraft. This, in turn, causes the wings to produce more lift than if the CG was located more rearward. So, a more forward CG also increases stall speed.
3. **Wing condition**\
   Snow, ice or frost accumulation on the wing’s surface not only changes the shape of the wing, disrupting the airflow, but also increases weight and drag, all of which will increase stall speed.
4. **Weather/turbulence**\
   The unpredictable nature of turbulence encounters can significantly and suddenly cause an aircraft to stall at a higher airspeed than the same aircraft in stable conditions. This occurs when a vertical gust changes the direction of the relative wind and abruptly increases the angle of attack.
5. **Use of high-lift devices**\
   The extension of flaps increases drag, it also increases the wing’s ability to produce lift, thereby reducing the stall speed.

### Types of Stalls
There are three *basic* types of stalls that will normally be practiced during training: **Power-off**, **Power-on,** and **Accelerated Stalls**
1. Power-off stalls are practiced to simulate the conditions and aircraft configuration you will most likely encounter during a normal landing approach. 
2. Power-on stalls are normally encountered during takeoff, climb-out, and go-arounds when the pilot fails to maintain proper control due to premature flap retraction or excessive nose-high trim. 
3. To help you understand how stalls may occur at higher than normal stall speed, your instructor may demonstrate accelerated stalls and show you associated recovery techniques. 

> [!CAUTION]
> Uncoordinated, or crossed-control, inputs can be very dangerous when operating near a stall. One type of stall, sometimes referred to as the **crossed-control stall** is most likely to occur when a pilot tries to compensate for overshooting a runway during a turn from base to final while on landing approach.

![Cross-controlled Stalls](/images/crosscontrolstall.png "Cross-controlled Stalls")

### Stall Recognition
1. **A “mushy” feeling in the flight controls**  
    As an aircraft approaches a stall, one of the first indications is often a “mushy” feeling in the flight controls and less control effect as the aircraft’s speed decreases. This reduction in control effectiveness occurs primarily because of reduced airflow over the flight control surfaces, making the aircraft respond more slowly to pilot inputs.
    
2. **Loss of revolutions per minute (RPM)**  
    In fixed-pitch propeller airplanes, a loss of revolutions per minute (RPM) may become noticeable during power-on conditions as the aircraft nears a stall. At the same time, there is usually a reduction in the sound of air flowing along the fuselage, which is another indication that airspeed is decreasing.
    
3. **Buffeting, uncontrollable pitching, or vibrations**  
    Just before the stall occurs, buffeting, uncontrollable pitching, or vibrations may begin. These sensations warn the pilot that the airflow over the wings is becoming increasingly unstable and that the aircraft is close to entering a stalled condition.
    
4. **A sudden changes in direction/speed or a sinking feeling**
    Detecting these early signs of an impending stall allows the pilot to make appropriate corrections before the stall fully develops.


### Stall Recovery
1. **Decrease the angle of attack**\
   Depending on of the type of aircraft, you might find that a different amount of forward pressure on the control wheel is required. Too little forward movement may not be enough to regain lift; too much may impose a negative load on the wing, hindering recovery. 
2. *Smoothly* apply **maximum** allowable power. \
   If you are not already at maximum allowable power, increase the throttle to minimize altitude loss and increase airspeed. 
3. Adjust the power as required. \
   As the airplane recovers, maintain coordinated flight while adjusting the power to a normal level.

## Spins
> **A spin may be defined as an aggravated\* stall, which results in the airplane descending in a helical, or corkscrew, path.**

 <sub>\**"aggravated" simply means that one wing is more stalled than the other wing.*</sub>

### Causes of Spins
**A stalled aircraft is a prerequisite for a spin.**

A spin is an uncoordinated maneuver with the wings **unequally** stalled.  In this case, the wing that is more completely stalled drops before the other, and the nose of the aircraft yaws in the direction of the low wing. The upgoing, lesser-stalled wing experiences more lift and less drag. The opposite, more-stalled wing is forced down and back due to less lift and increased drag. 

![During a spin, the wings are unequally stalled, which sustains the rolling and yawing motions of the spin.](/images/spincauses.png "During a spin, the wings are unequally stalled, which sustains the rolling and yawing motions of the spin.")

Typically, the cause of an inadvertent spin is exceeding the critical angle of attack while performing an uncoordinated maneuver, **normally caused by either too much or not enough rudder control for the amount of aileron being used.**

### Types of Spins
![Types of Spins](/images/spintypes.png "Types of Spins")

1. **Erect Spin**\
   The most common type is the upright, or erect spin, which is characterized by a slightly nose down *rolling and yawing motion in the same direction.* 
2. **Inverted Spin**\
   In an inverted spin, the aircraft is spinning upside down with *yaw and roll occurring in opposite directions.* Inverted spins are most likely to occur during aerobatic maneuvers. 
3. **Flat Spin**\
   In a flat spin, the aircraft simply yaws about its vertical axis with a pitch attitude approximately level with the horizon. Although it sounds fairly benign, recovery is usually very difficult or impossible except in specialized aerobatic aircraft, *which can be the most deadly.* 

### Weight and Balance Considerations
* **Weight**\
  Heavier weights generally result in slow spin rates initially; but, as the spin progresses, heavier weights tend to cause an increasing spin rate and longer recovery time. 
* **Distribution**
	* CG forward tends to be more stall and spin resistant than aft CG.
	* Flat Spins are more likely to occur when the CG is aft.

### Spin Phases
1. **Incipient Spin**\
   The incipient spin is that portion of a spin from the time the airplane stalls and rotation starts until the spin is fully developed, *and the aircraft is stalled and is increasing in pitch, roll, and yaw rates*. The incipient phase for most aircraft is typically anywhere from one-half to two turns (about 4~6 seconds). At about the half-turn point, the airplane is pointed almost straight down but the angle of attack is usually above that of the stall because of the inclined flight path. As the one-turn point approaches, the nose might come back up and the angle of attack continues to increase.

2. **Fully Developed Spin**\
   The fully developed spin begins somewhere in the second turn as the airplane continues to rotate and the angular rotation rates, airspeed, and vertical speed become *stabilized with a nearly vertical flight path.* 

3. **Recovery**\
   Spin recovery is the final stage of the spin that occurs when anti-spin forces overcome pro-spin forces. The FAA has in an Advisory Circular (61-67C) a spin recovery technique, and flight instructors have taken this technique and broken it down into a four-step acronym called **P.A.R.E.** * 
    1. **P** – Power reduction. Pull the *throttle back to idle.*
    2. **A** – Ailerons. *Neutralize the ailerons*. Now, think back to what we said earlier: the aircraft must be stalled and in an aggravated condition. Where is that aggravation coming from? Usually aileron displacement. So, step two: ailerons neutral.
    3. **R** – Rudder. *Rudder opposite* the direction of rotation until rotation stops.
    4. **E** – Elevator. *Elevators forward, approximately the neutral position*, to reduce the angle of attack.
