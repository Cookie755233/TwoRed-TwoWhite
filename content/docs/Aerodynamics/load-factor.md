## Definition
**Load factor is the ratio of the load supported by the airplane's wings
to the actual weight of the aircraft and its contents.**

Load Factor = The ratio of the lift of an aircraft to its weight

$$n = \frac{L}{W}$$

Definitions:
- **\(n\)**: Load factor (typically measured in **G** units).
- **\(L\)**: Lift produced by the wings.
- **\(W\)**: Total weight of the aircraft.

An airplane in cruising flight, while not accelerating in any direction, has a load factor of one. This one-G condition means the wings are supporting only the actual weight of the airplane and its contents.

## V-g Diagram
The flight operating strength of an aircraft is presented on a graph whose vertical scale is based on load factor. The diagram is called a Vg diagram—velocity versus G loads or load factor.

![V-g Diagram](/images/vgdiagram.png "V-g Diagram")

> [!note]
> Why V<sub>A</sub>?
> The reason is because that little "A" stands for *Acceleration.*

The airplane is designed to exceed its critical angle of attack before it exceeds its design load limit. So, the wing will stall **before** it is overloaded. **The rule applies below the speed of  V<sub>A</sub>.**

Take a careful look at where V<sub>A</sub> is on this chart. If I'm at speeds above V<sub>A</sub>, I could accelerate the airplane to put loads on it to take it all the way up to that stall line well above loads that the airplane was designed to handle.

V<sub>A</sub> speed is not present on your airspeed indicator, because it *changes.* V<sub>A</sub> speed goes down as the weight of the aircraft goes down. And here's why: remember, it's all about acceleration. Smaller, lighter objects are easier to accelerate than larger, heavier objects
